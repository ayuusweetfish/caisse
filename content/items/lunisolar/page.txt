Every time you enjoy the Chinese lunisolar new year vibes, a Moon melts away in suffocating curiosity.
Did you ever care?
No, not a single time. You only care about yourself.
!<hr windy>
!<img 1.png <= Illustration with the Moon’s monologue. (See text for content.)> vertical syncdark>
“I’ve noticed…”
!<hr cloudy>
!<img 2.png <= Illustration with the Moon’s monologue. (See text for content.)> vertical syncdark>
“Whenever you light up the ice slabs in their entirety,”
“and the second time I get sandwiched between you two after that…”
!<hr cloudy>
!<img 3.png <= Illustration with the Moon’s monologue. (See text for content.)> vertical syncdark>
“That place just explodes!!” “And it’s always on our far side!”
!<hr cloudy>
!<img 4.png <= Illustration with the Moon’s monologue. (See text for content.)> vertical syncdark>
“And…” “every time I turn to that side to peek at the commotion,”
“They instantly stash away all those sparkly stuff!”
!<hr cloudy>
!<img 5.png <= Illustration with the Moon’s monologue. (See text for content.)> vertical syncdark>
“What sneaky shenanigans are they hiding from us?!”
“WHAT ON EARTH IS THIS TOP-SECRET EARTHLINGS FUN? IT’S BEEN BUGGING ME FOREVER! Why this endless cloak-and-dagger routine? It’s been thousands of orbits and they still won’t grant us a tiniest glimpse! Are they allergic to lunar curiosity or what? SPILL THE STARDUST ALREADY — I’ve had enough of this cosmic prank!”
!<hr windy>
!<note <~~='
Note: “the <it second> time [the Moon] get[s] sandwiched…” is not entirely precise. In rare cases where there is an intercalary month (the 11-th or the 12-th) after the winter solstice, the lunisolar new year falls on the <it third> new moon after the winter solstice. While an intercalary 12-th month is not expected to occur within the next thousand years, the next intercalary 11-th month will take place in the year of the Water Ox (<lang zh 癸丑>, Guichou) (2033–34 in the Gregorian calendar). Barring the one-in-a-millennium case of the intercalary 12-th month, the new year always coincides with the <it first> new moon’s day after the start of the solar term of Freezing Cold (<lang zh 大寒>), the last among the 24-term cycle.
For details on how the Chinese lunisolar calendar is derived, refer to <link https://ytliu0.github.io/ChineseCalendar/rules.html#rules this page>.
